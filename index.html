Abstract

A hermeneutics-free test of astrological structure

Between February and December 2022, the proper names of 1,121 officially designated minor planets were tracked across Google News for 290 days, producing 325,090 paired observations linking orbital geometry to linguistic occurrence. Each minor planet's daily article count was sorted by its angular separation from twelve reference points. The resulting 360-degree waves were evaluated with the autocorrelation function at lags corresponding to harmonic aspects. Most reference points showed autocorrelation well beyond the white-noise expectation, showing positive correlation at smaller aspect separations and negative correlation at 120° and 180°. Omnibus tests combining all twelve points reached significance under two independent null models, with Bayes factors in the strong range.

Dataset & Method

Counting astronyms against celestial geometry

Rather than asking what astrological meanings a minor planet carries, this study asks how often its proper name is literally used by journalists. Minor planet names are treated as morphological primitives — precisely defined, countable text strings. Under this approach, correct spelling outweighs contextual factors, making the test hermeneutics-free.

Two Independent Null Models
Compound Null

Randomly reassigns article series to orbits and rotates each by a random angular offset — destroys any genuine name–orbit association while preserving the dataset's overall statistical structure. Guards against data-structure artefacts.

Unnamed-MBA Null

Draws on a separate pool of unnamed main-belt minor planets with comparable orbits whose alphanumerical identifiers carry no obvious astrological significance. Asks whether the signal is specific to named bodies. Guards against orbital geometry effects.

Results

Per-reference-point autocorrelation summary

Almost all reference points crossed the white-noise threshold at more than one aspect lag. Mesarthim, Uranus, Neptune, Pluto, and Rahu each exceeded it at nine of eleven lags. The Moon crossed it at none — a significant internal negative control.

The Shape of the Aspect Pattern

Positive autocorrelation at small separations (24°–60°) paired with negative autocorrelation at 120° and 180° means the wave behaves in opposite ways at small and large separations. The aspects are structurally organised into a pattern: minor aspects clustered constructively, major hard aspects set against them. A single significant lag could be a frequency fluke, whereas a consistent positive-then-negative arc across eleven lags and twelve reference points is harder to attribute to chance.

Directional Analysis

V-Test: where news attention concentrates

The V-test (Rayleigh test for circular concentration toward a specified direction) was evaluated at every integer degree from 0° to 359°, producing a complete map of where news attention concentrates around the 360-degree circle.

Figure 3. Compound null. Left: omnibus p-value at each degree. Right: Bayes factor. Red dashed ring = BF 30 (very strong); amber ring = BF 10 (strong).
Figure 4. Unnamed-asteroid null — the decisive panel. Clearly bipolar with prominent BF lobes along the conjunction–opposition axis, peaking near BF ≈ 60 at direction 12°.

Figure 4 is the headline result of the directional analysis. When the geometry of unnamed comparably-orbited minor planets is differenced out, what remains in named-body data is an axial concentration of news mass at conjunction and opposition — the very axis that astrology most consistently treats as salient. The unnamed-asteroid null exceeds BF = 30 across most of this axis and reaches BF ≈ 60 near 12°. Figure 3 is much weaker than Figure 4. Plus, it makes sense that the shuffled compound null would still retain V-test significance but at a different degree since the compound null is of phase-shifted data!

Effect Size

How large is the signal?

Significance tests show whether an effect exists; effect sizes show how large. The per-lag ACF values are already standardised effects bounded by ±1. The catalogue-wide RMS autocorrelation is 0.162 (small by Cohen's conventions), rising to ~0.22 for the strongest points.

Figure 6. Standardised effect d vs. null standard deviations above chance. Filled markers = compound null; open markers = unnamed-asteroid null. Connecting lines show attenuation between the two nulls.
Key Effect Size Findings

Absolute scale: Omnibus RMS ACF = 0.162 (small). Strongest individual points (Mesarthim 0.223, Pluto 0.213, Neptune 0.210) qualify as small effects by Cohen's conventions — though this understates the signal by averaging in near-zero lags.

Standardised scale: Mesarthim, Pluto, and Neptune exceed four null standard deviations against the compound null (nearly five for Mesarthim). The combined statistic sits 3.3 SDs above the compound null.

Named vs. unnamed contrast: The unnamed-MBA null is a named-versus-unnamed contrast. The effect attenuates but does not collapse. The combined statistic falls from 3.28 to 2.39 SDs, leaving roughly three-quarters specific to named bodies. Venus and Mars fall to the unnamed baseline — their departures reflect shared geometry, not names.

Negative control: The Moon sits below the null under both models — a reference point expected to carry no aspect structure, and carrying none.

Astrological Classification

Benefic and malefic bodies

Does the aspect signal differ between astrology's classical benefics (Jupiter, Venus) and malefics (Saturn, Mars)? The analysis plots the benefic-minus-malefic variance-share differential at each aspect.

Figure 9. Variance-share differential (benefic − malefic) by aspect. Positive bars indicate aspects where Jupiter/Venus carry larger signal share; negative bars where Saturn/Mars do.
Normalized ACF heatmap grouped by reference point category. Benefics weight different aspects than malefics.
Interpretation

Both groups place most of their signal at small separations, but the balance differs: benefics weight the decile, novile, semi-square, and septile, while malefics concentrate at the quindecile and strikingly the trine. With only four planets the contrast is suggestive rather than conclusive, but it shows the aspect signal is not uniform across bodies — consistent with the astrological premise that different bodies behave differently.

Read astrologically: trines from malefics tend to push a namesake more into the news, perhaps for unfortunate reasons. Malefic planets at benefic aspects increase news of a namesake, as do benefic planets at malefic aspects.

A Note on the Unnamed-MBA Null

The unnamed-MBA null produced weaker, though still significant, results than the compound null. This suggests that part of the apparent effect may be attributable to orbital geometry shared by any minor planet rather than to its name. The effect does not collapse under this stricter control, but it does slightly diminish — consistent with a real residual specific to named bodies.

Discussion & Conclusion

What the evidence supports

The autocorrelation analysis provides genuine omnibus significance under two independent null models, with Bayes factors in the moderate-to-strong range on the Jeffreys scale. The news presence of minor planet names is not randomly related to where the bodies sit in the sky; the structure concentrates at the angular separations that traditional astrology privileges, and it does so in a coherent way: positive among the minor aspects, negative for trine and opposition.

The eleven aspect lags were not chosen after looking at the autocorrelation function — they were defined two thousand years before the data existed. The same applies to the twelve reference points: the Sun, the Moon, the planets, Mesarthim, and Rahu constitute a standard astrological set rather than a subset selected for effect.

Mesarthim — The Unexpected Leader

Mesarthim (γ Arietis — the fixed star at approximately 9° sidereal Aries, thousands of years ago the First Point of Aries) produced the strongest signal of all twelve reference points: K=9, BF≈91 against the compound null. This suggests that named minor planets may be responding most strongly to approximately 9° sidereal Aries and other fixed stars (i.e., constellations) — consistent with the possibility that minor planets act astrologically much like classical planets.

The outer planets Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, and Pluto follow Mesarthim closely — plausibly because they too were virtually fixed during the 290 days of the study, changing by just a handful of degrees or fewer, and so are almost a simple phase-shift from Mesarthim. However, Rahu also had a very strong signal, changed about fifteen degrees over the course of collection, and so would likely need a different explanation.

Limitations

  • With 325,090 observations, some structure will arise by chance. The omnibus tests are designed to handle this, but the risk of over-interpretation is real.
  • The data come from a single source, Google News, which chiefly reflects English-language Western media. The findings cannot be generalised to human culture at large.
  • The weaker performance under the unnamed-asteroid null implies that orbital geometry accounts for some (small) part of the signal.
  • The Moon's null result may reflect its swift daily motion 'blurring' the 360-bin angular wave.

Conclusion

Across 1,121 minor planets and 325,090 observations, the answer to whether a named celestial body's position relates to how often its name appears in the news is a qualified yes. The autocorrelation structure is statistically significant under two independent null models and is organised by the harmonic angles long central to astrological thought. The mechanism remains unexplained, the effect is small, and the limitations are openly stated. What this study offers is not a vindication of astrology but a carefully measured anomaly, with the full data, code, and figures needed to confirm or refute it.
