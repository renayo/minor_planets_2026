Minor Planet Orbits and Astronyms in the News

Renay Oshop · Anton Coops · 2026

Across 290 consecutive days of Google News coverage in 2022, the 1,121 officially-named minor planets show statistically structured correlation with twelve reference points at the harmonic angles long treated as salient in astrological tradition. The signal is significant under both a phase-shift null and a stricter unnamed-asteroid null, and approximately three-quarters of it is specific to bodies that bear names.

Abstract

What the study tests, and what it finds

For each named minor planet, the daily count of Google News articles containing the planet's proper name was logged across 290 consecutive days in 2022. Those counts were paired with the daily angular separation between the planet and each of twelve reference points: the Sun, the Moon, Mercury through Pluto, the fixed star FPOA (the First Point of Aries), and the mean north lunar node Rahu. The resulting 360-degree distributions of name mass were analysed for the harmonic angular structure predicted by classical aspect doctrine — autocorrelation at the eleven canonical aspect lags (24°, 30°, 36°, 40°, 45°, 51°, 60°, 72°, 90°, 120°, 180°), Rayleigh / V-test directional concentration, and shape comparison with the Gauquelin diurnal sectors.

The autocorrelation test combining all twelve reference points reaches significance under both null models. Under the compound (phase-shift) null the joint ACF² statistic sits 3.3 standard deviations above the null mean, with Monte-Carlo p ≈ 0.019 and Bayes factor ≈ 27. Under the stricter unnamed main-belt asteroid (MBA) null the effect attenuates by about 27% but does not collapse: p ≈ 0.040, BF ≈ 13. The signal is carried mostly by FPOA, Pluto, Neptune, Uranus, Saturn, Rahu, and Jupiter; the Moon, with a negative standardised effect, serves as an internal negative control.

The result is small in absolute correlation (RMS |ACF| ≈ 0.17, "small" by Cohen's scale) but coherent: the same positive-at-small-aspects, negative-at-large-aspects shape repeats across eleven lags and nine of twelve reference points, and a small pattern repeated that many times lands far out in a tight null distribution. The Rahu finding — that a mathematical point with no physical body behaves geometrically like the slow named outer planets — is consistent with a geometric reading of the catalogue-wide signal.

Method

Dataset and reference points

Google News article counts were logged for each of 1,121 named minor planets across 290 consecutive days (15 February through 1 December 2022). For every day the geocentric angular separation between each minor planet and each reference point was computed from JPL Horizons, then encoded in the Sun-relative form (body − Sun) mod 360 to remove overlapping coverage effects across closely-spaced reference points.

The two null models

Compound (phase-shift) null

The same 1,121 named bodies, but with each body's daily article wave rotated by a uniformly random integer phase shift in [1, 359]. Removes any common time-of-day pattern across the catalogue.

This is the looser of the two nulls — it preserves the orbital geometry of every named body and tests only whether the *temporal alignment* between the wave and the reference points is meaningful.

Unnamed-MBA null

Same procedure, but applied to a pool of unnamed main-belt asteroids with similar orbital geometry. Removes both the time-of-day pattern AND any geometry common to numbered minor planets in the asteroid belt.

This is the stricter null. Any signal that survives here is specific to *being named* — to the catalogue of bodies that bear human-conferred proper names.

Diagnostic

The dominant axis in the catalogue is speed of reference point

Across the eleven moving reference points (the planets, the Sun, the Moon, and Rahu), the per-SP Ljung–Box Q — the canonical statistic for cumulative deviation from a white-noise autocorrelation — is well described as a log-linear function of the body's mean absolute daily motion. The single fixed-regime point (FPOA, essentially stationary at precession rate ~10⁻⁵ °/day) sits in a separate regime and was excluded from the fit.

Mean absolute daily motion (degrees per day, log scale) versus per-SP Ljung–Box Q across all twelve reference points. Among the eleven moving bodies, a log-linear regression gives Q = 67.7 − 86.8 · log₁₀(speed), R² = 0.929 (Pearson r = −0.964, p ≈ 2·10⁻⁶, Spearman ρ = −0.973), RMSE = 23.5 LB_Q units, with a 95% CI for the slope of [−105, −69]. Each decade of faster motion costs ~87 units of Q. The white-noise reference thresholds (95% at Q ≈ 19.7, 99.9% at Q ≈ 31.3) and a 95% prediction band around the trendline are shown. The FPOA, at precession rate, sits well below the planet-trend extrapolation (which would predict Q ≈ 451 at that speed) — this fixed-regime point captures a different kind of signal (zodiacal anchoring) than the moving bodies' speed-driven relative-geometry signal.
Two-regime decomposition. The catalogue-wide signal decomposes naturally into a speed-dependent relative-geometry component (which scales at roughly 87 units of Q per decade of body speed and dominates the planet results) and a zodiacal-anchoring component (captured by the FPOA, which carries strong signal despite zero relative motion). Rahu, at Saturn's effective speed (~0.05 °/day), lies exactly on the planet trendline — it is geometrically a slow-mover by virtue of its node motion, with nothing extra beyond what its speed predicts. The FPOA's position, by contrast, departs from the planet extrapolation, serving as a fixed celestial anchor whose signal comes from the distribution of named-body longitudes themselves rather than from relative geometry.
Benefics vs malefics

The classical benefic–malefic contrast

Traditional astrology divides the visible planets into benefics (Jupiter, Venus) and malefics (Saturn, Mars). If that distinction carries any signature in the autocorrelation profile, it should appear as a systematic difference between the two groups at the harmonic lags. The diverging-bar plot below shows, for each aspect lag, the difference between the mean benefic ACF and the mean malefic ACF: positive bars are aspects where the benefics carry more of their joint signal than the malefics; negative bars are the reverse.

Per-aspect difference (mean benefic ACF − mean malefic ACF) normalised by each row's joint Σ ACF². Positive bars indicate aspects where the benefic pair (Jupiter, Venus) carries a larger share of its joint signal than the malefic pair (Saturn, Mars); negative bars indicate the reverse. The classical benefic–malefic distinction does not appear as a clear axis in the data — the differences are modest and inconsistent in sign across the eleven astrological lags. The dominant axis in the catalogue is body speed (slow movers vs. fast movers), not benefic vs. malefic.

Directional

V-test landscape at every direction under both nulls

The Rayleigh V-test evaluates concentration around a specified direction. Computing it at every integer degree from 0° to 359° for each of the twelve reference points produces a "landscape" of directional preference. The polar plots below show the omnibus T = Σ d² combined across all twelve SPs, with the observed curve in colour.

Figure 3 · Compound null

Twelve-SP V-test omnibus landscape against the compound null. The observed curve extends well beyond the null envelope at directions clustered around 0°/180° (conjunction-opposition axis) for the slow-mover cluster, and around 60°/120° for Rahu. Note that this is a different, much weaker, but significant angle than for the unnamed-asteroid set as null (see Figure 4). This is sensical: the compound null dataset retains the structure of the real test data, but it has been shifted — so the directions at which the two nulls find residual concentration are not expected to coincide.

Figure 4 · Unnamed-MBA null

Same analysis against the unnamed-MBA null over 12 SPs. The observed curve stays above the null envelope across most of the circle, with tightest separation at the conjunction-opposition axis. BF reaches roughly 48 at the omnibus level (versus the BF=30 "very strong" threshold). The stricter null compresses the envelope, so the contrast is smaller in absolute terms but preserves the directional pattern.

Discussion

What this means, and what it does not

The catalogue-level signal is statistically robust: it survives the strict named-vs-unnamed contrast, it does not depend on Bonferroni correction across reference points (the omnibus aggregates are single combined tests), and the headline Bayes factors sit in the "strong" range of the Jeffreys scale under both null models. The most plausible reading is that the data show structured catalogue-wide correlation between minor planet article counts and the angular geometry between minor planets and twelve reference points.

What it does not say. The absolute autocorrelation is small (RMS |ACF| ≈ 0.16), so the practical effect on any individual body's article count is modest. The unnamed-MBA control removes most but not all of the geometric confound; the remaining roughly three-quarters of the effect that is named-specific is consistent with multiple potential causal mechanisms related to the act of journalism.

The FPOA — itself a non-body reference, fixed in tropical longitude at the precession rate — establishes that the zodiacal-anchoring component of the catalogue signal is real and independently detectable at fixed celestial positions.

Reproducibility

Code, data, and reading

All scripts, data files, intermediate CSVs, and final figures are in the public GitHub repository. The pipeline runs end-to-end in about ten minutes on a modern laptop at the 5,000-iteration Monte-Carlo resolution used in this revision.
